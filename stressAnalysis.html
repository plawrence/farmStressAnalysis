NGP Wheat Farming Price and Precipitation Stress Analysis¶

In the code below, I document my process of analyzing historical stressors for large-scale wheat farms in the Northern Great Plains. Specifically, I want to know how resilient farmers are in the face of the common major stressors they face.

Hypothesis 1: The major driving stressors in large-scale agricultural systems are drought, and the nitrogen-to-wheat price ratio. Weed/pest pressure and spatial variability have a much lower economic impact on farms overall.

(Answer using survey and interview data)

Hypothesis 2: Farmers have become much less economically resilient and much more vulnerable to climatic changes since 1960.

  • 2a: Farmers have become more reliant on external inputs. Thus, as prices for inputs and commodities have experienced greater fluctuations, the economic stability of farms has decreased. (Historical Data)
  • 2b: Farmers currently have few options for moderating the effects of economic stressors (nitrogen-to-wheat price ratio) or the stress associated with unpredictable drought. (Primarily survey, some interview)
  • 2c: Farmers utilize only a limited number of information sources for learning about agronomic alternatives that would increase their adaptability. (Primarily interview, some survey)

Hypothesis 3: The temporal proximity to these stressors drives farmers' choices related to adaptability. As the time since experiencing a stressor increases, adaptability to that source of stress decreases.

(Interview)

Part 4: How do farmers go from seeing a problem, to seeking and implementing a solution? Note: I am generating theory here from my data, rather than testing a hypothesis.

(Interview - inductively coded)

I hypothesize that as the time since stress increases, farmers become less concerned with adaptations to avoid that source of stress. For example, if there was a drought last year, farmers would be highly interested in alternative management strategies that could help them avoid the impacts of reduced moisture. As the time since that drought increased, they would become more and more complacent, perhaps focusing on other stressors that were more immediate (especially given their typical 1-2 yr planning time horizons).

With this knowledge, it would be possible both to understand the physical stressors that may drive adaptability, but also to take advantage of the associated psychological processes. If it had been a long time since a drought, but the N-wheat price ratio was relatively high, then trying to promote alternative management strategies with the stated goal of drought mitigation would be pointless. Instead, it would make more sense to emphasize the benefits of a practice such as crop rotation for the purpose of reducing N inputs, rather than as a water use minimization strategy.

The final part of this analysis is intended to feed into my assessment of resilience. How resilient are farmers to drought and changes in prices. Do they have many options available? If not (which appears to be the case), how could we promote the use of alternative crops/management that simultaneously fit our goals for sustainability? What would be the most effective avenues or information channels to promote those alternatives? This final bit gets into the sociological methods.

The analysis was performed in Python, using the pandas and matplotlib packages

To perform the analysis, I draw upon wheat and Nitrogen price data from the USDA, Palmer z-index (a drought index) data from the National Climatic Data Center (NCDC), and census data from the USDA National Ag Statistics Service (NASS). In order to place the impact of the N-Wheat price ratio deviations into context, it would be helpful to know the average farmers' dependence on [exposure to] N, relative to the total yields on their farm. If their dependence on N is high, this would have a lower impact for smaller acreages than larger acreages. A smaller-scale farmer would have the same relative exposure on a per-acre basis, but a much lower exposure over the entire farm acreage.

To get a handle on this, we need to know average farm size, and the quantity of N applied per acre. We will assume that yields do not vary much by farm size. Obviously, the data probably don't exist to assess the amounts of applied N per acre by farm size. However, we're more interested in average exposures to N-Wheat price ratio fluctuations, so averages may be sufficient.

Conclusion - Hypothesis 2a.

The twin stressors of drought and the Nitrogen-to-wheat price ratio have been variable since the mid-20th century. However, the stress from drought has a much higher variance and is more unpredictable. Since 2007, its impact on overall stress has been minimal. In contrast, the N-W price ratio has steadily increased since 1974 and has remained at unprecedented levels since the mid-2000s.

Hypothesis 2b: Farmers currently have few options for moderating the effects of economic stressors (nitrogen-to-wheat price ratio) or the stress associated with unpredictable drought.
